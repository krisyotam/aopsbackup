Exploring the Riemann Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function, denoted by \( \zeta(s) \), is a function of complex variable \( s \) that plays a crucial role in number theory and mathematical analysis. It is defined as:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} \]

This function converges for all \( \Re(s) > 1 \), and it can be extended to other values of \( s \) using analytic continuation. A famous conjecture related to the Riemann zeta function is the Riemann Hypothesis, which posits that all non-trivial zeros of the zeta function have real part equal to \( \frac{1}{2} \).

We can also express \( \zeta(s) \) in terms of the Euler product, which highlights its connection to prime numbers:

\[ \zeta(s) = \prod_{p \, \text{prime}} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^s}\right)^{-1} \]

For example, for \( s = 2 \), we get the famous result:

\[ \zeta(2) = \frac{\pi^2}{6} \]

Thus, the value of \( \zeta(2) \) is related to \( \pi \), showcasing the deep connections between different areas of mathematics.
